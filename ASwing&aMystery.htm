Year 8 Homework Week 1

A Swing and a Mystery

Mrs McCarthy had been looking forward to a quiet lunchtime. She was usually not a stressed person, but her pupils had given her a morning that would have tried anyone’s patience. It had rained throughout, and her class had been intense.

First there had been Felix Duncan, who had heard the Crazy Frog song for the first time over the weekend and kept singing it at his classmates until they told him to stop, and then sang it even louder. Then there was Summer Jones, who had been in one of her haughty moods and managed to argue with three of her classmates over the course of three lessons. Perhaps most irritatingly of all, Miss Haq from the class next door was off sick, and all morning she had had to keep popping in to remind the Year 5’s to be nice to the supply teacher.

But now, having collected her lunch from the staffroom and having extricated herself from a long-winded conversation with the caretaker, Mrs McCarthy had twenty whole minutes to enjoy a quiet cup of tea and the sandwich that she had made herself.

All was serenity, all was peace, until Mrs McCarthy raised the sandwich to her mouth.

“Miss! Miss!” came a voice from the corridor outside.

Mrs McCarthy exhaled deeply and put down her sandwich with a bubbling fury. She was irritated at being called ‘Miss’. She was in her mid-fifties, and hadn’t been a Miss since 2003. She was piqued by having one of her pupils standing in the corridor shouting. She was incensed about being disturbed during her lunchtime, when she wasn’t even on duty.

She opened the door to see a teary and red-faced Summer Jones. Mrs McCarthy’s temper melted almost immediately. No matter how many interruptions she’d faced today she just couldn’t get angry at a crying child. It was how she’d managed to stay a primary school teacher for so long.

Summer was doubled over, holding her knee in pain. A fall, Mrs McCarthy assumed, probably from the swings. She had told the pupils not to use the outdoor equipment after the heavy rain, but of course they’d used it anyway. Mrs McCarthy lowered herself to speak to the sobbing Summer.

“Let me look,” Mrs McCarthy said, patiently. Summer removed her hand. It was covered in blood. Summer’s knee was covered in a nasty gash.

Mrs McCarthy made a quick examination. “It looks worse than it is,” she said, trying to give Summer a smile. “Don’t panic.” She went to the first aid cupboard, fetched a large plaster and disinfectant spray. She began to treat Summer.

“Now, how did this happen?” she asked the girl.

“I was pushed,” the girl responded through tears, “off the swings.”

Mrs McCarthy wasn’t sure if Summer was lying. “Pushed? Are you sure? You didn’t just fall?”

“No Miss,” Summer said, “I was pushed from behind.”

“By a group of people?”

“No, just one. They pushed me off and then ran away. Everyone else was queuing for the slide. Or still in the lunch hall.”

“Did you see who?” Mrs McCarthy asked.

Summer shook her head. “They ran away.”

As Mrs McCarthy pasted the plaster, she considered. As sweet as she looked, Summer wasn’t always a well-behaved child. That morning she had been told off for saying malicious things to three different pupils. Each of the three had left the class red-faced and embarrassed. They all had a reason to be angry at Summer and were all at lunch at the same time.

Mrs McCarthy thought about the three suspects.

Magnus Winston was a quiet boy. He’d spend his lunchtimes playing chess, usually against himself. There were picnic benches near the swings where he’d sometimes play chess on a dry day. Otherwise, he’d be in the hall.

Saffiya Patel was a natural show-off, who loved to exhibit her dancing and gymnastics skills, whether that was hanging upside down from the slide, or leaping off the picnic tables. She could always be found where the biggest crowd was.

Lucie Barnes was a force of nature. She had no patience and hated sitting still or standing in lines. She spent most of her lunchtime alternately racing around the playground, playing games that involved hitting things with sticks, or using the school buildings for parkour.

When Mrs McCarthy asked the three pupils, all of them denied they’d pushed Summer. But they also promised they hadn’t seen the other two. This meant that one of them was at each location. Anyone coming from the slide or hall would have been obvious to Summer. If she was pushed from behind, it had to be from the picnic benches.

Mrs McCarthy knew the identity of the person who had attacked Summer. Do you?
