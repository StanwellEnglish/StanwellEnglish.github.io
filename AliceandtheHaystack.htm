Year 7 Homework Week 1

Alice and the Haystack

Alice had been wandering the open countryside for what felt like hours, when she came to a wheatfield which had been freshly harvested. But all of the hay appeared to have been piled up in one corner by the road, so that Alice was dwarfed by a colossal haystack the height of a streetlamp.

As she looked up at the enormous pile of hay, she heard sobbing. She walked around the base of the haystack, until she found a woman in a dusty red dress weeping into her apron.

With red and swollen eyes, the woman looked up as Alice approached. "Oh, it's no good. I shall never find it!" she sniffled, dabbing at her nose with a corner of her dress.

Alice's eyes grew wide. "What is it?" she asked, her voice filled with concern.

The woman took a deep, shuddering breath. "My sewing needle," she said, her voice cracking with despair. "I'm a dressmaker, you see, and without it, I cannot make a single stitch!"

Alice wanted to be as helpful as she could, so she was about to ask, "Wherever did you lose it?" but then remembered that whenever her own mother asked her this, it made her fly into a rage. If she'd known where she lost something, it wouldn't be lost.

"It's lost in this haystack!" the woman told Alice, weeping again. "Oh, I shall never find it, even with all the help."

Alice was wondering what the woman meant by help, but her thoughts were disturbed by a sudden blast from a bugle, coming from the top of the haystack. Alice looke up to see, half buried in the hay, a cockerel standing to attention, blowing into a horn that rang out loud and long across the fields.

"That's the end of day call," said the dressmaker. "It's time for all the animals to show what they've found. Oh I do hope they ve found my needle.

The haystack began to make all sorts of noises, including muffled sound of barks, squeaks, and the occasional roar of laughter. Some sort of creatures were moving deep within the hay. Suddenly Alice saw, scurrying out from the bottom of the hay a tiny mole, who was wearing spectacles almost as large as its whole body. "No luck today," she said. "Try again tomorrow."

"Not so fast!" cried another voice, and the head of a ferret popped out. "I've found it, I've found it!" he said. With another wriggle, the ferret's whole body came through, and Alice saw that rather than a needle, he was proudly holding up his own tail.

"That's your tail again," said the dressmaker, glumly. "You need to be careful you don't pull it off."

"Needn't worry about that," the effervescent ferret chuckled, "if I pulled it off, you could always sew it back on for me."

"No, I couldn't!" blubbed the dressmaker, "I don't have my needle!"

"But surely you have more than one? And if not, couldn't you just make another needle?" Alice asked, again only trying to be helpful.

"And how would you go about doing that then?" the Ferret queried, his eyebrow raised.

"Well, you just..." began Alice, and then realised that she didn't have the first idea how needles were actually made. Presumably there was some large factory out there that made only needles, or perhaps it did both needles and other sorts of pins.

Before she could get much further with her reasoning, a trio of animals bundled up in a ball sprang from the top of the haystack. With a mix of yowls and barks they almost knocked the cockerel from his perch. They landed on the ground, unfolding into two ferocious bloodhounds and an exhausted looking fox. The ferret leapt between the dogs and the fox to separate their squabble.

"I've told you hounds before," said the ferret, "No fox hunting in the haystack!"

"But we're not in the haystack," one of the hounds said, grinning and looking at the fox with eager eyes.

"Cut it out," ordered the ferret, who seemed to be in charge. "That fox is the only reason we've made any progress at all."

Alice brightened at this. "You've made progress?" she asked.

"Oh yes," said the ferret, returning to his smile. "We've found something almost every day we ve been looking. Look!" He gestured towards the fence, where there was a motley collection of objects: a bicycle, the remnants of a scarecrow, some sort of rod, a euphonium and a false leg.

"You found all of these in the haystack?" asked Alice, wondering how some of the things could have ended up all together in the stack.

"Not to mention this bugle," called the cockerel in his faint voice from the top of the haystack.

"But no needle," wailed the dressmaker.

Alice looked at the menagerie of assistants the dressmaker had helping her, then at the odd objects they had all found. A thought came to her. "Do any of you actually know what a needle looks like?" she asked.

There was a pause. The animals began to look around, hoping someone else would answer first.

"Well, it's..." began one of the hounds, "it's like..."

"No," interrupted the other, "it's nothing like that."

"No," replied the first hound, his face falling. "I suppose it's not."

"I can help look if you like," Alice suggested, though she had no idea where to begin.

"Help look?" exclaimed the ferret indignantly. "But the end-of-day bugle has called! If you start looking now, you'll mess up our whole system!"

"I'm not sure the system has been terribly successful..." Alice began, but the creatures had already begun haughtily sticking their noses in the air, and walking away from her, the haystack and the wailing lady.
